The address that points at itself

When you build an app, your computer quietly runs a tiny private server just for you. The address localhost:3000 means "this very machine, door number 3000" — the number is called a port, and a port is just a numbered door. Your browser knocks on it like it would knock on any website, but the request never leaves your laptop.

And here's the part that explains your confused friend: localhost means "this computer" on every computer. It's like the word "home." When you say it, you mean your house. When your friend says it, they mean theirs. Send someone a localhost link and their browser knocks on their own machine — where your site has never existed. Hence the error.

The name itself is just two words glued together: local — this machine, right here — and host, any computer that serves something. Your laptop, hosting a website, for an audience of exactly one.

Why builders live here

Three reasons all real building happens on localhost first:

  • It's free. No hosting, no bills, no setup.
  • It's instant. Save a change, refresh, see it. The loop takes seconds.
  • It's private. Break things spectacularly. Nobody's watching.

This is exactly the loop you'll live in when you're vibe coding with Claude: it writes code, tells you "open http://localhost:3000," you look, you say "make the headline bigger," refresh, repeat. The widget's edit button was that loop, miniaturized. And now when Claude Code hands you a localhost link, you know exactly what it means — and why it works only for you.

Opening night

When the site is ready, you deploy: copy it onto an always-on server with a real address that DNS can find. Now everyone sees it — your friend included. Deploying doesn't delete your localhost, either. You keep both: the private draft on your machine, the public copy on the server.

But keep the widget's last lesson: after you deployed, your next edit showed up on localhost only, and the live site kept showing the old version until you deployed again. Live is a snapshot. Localhost is the living draft. Shipping is the act of copying one onto the other — and you decide when.

Classic mixup: you screen-share a demo and it looks great, then you paste the same http://localhost:3000 link in Slack and nobody can open it. Of course they can't — you sent them the word "home." Everyone who clicks lands at their own house. To share it, deploy it.

✓ Check yourself

1. You text a friend: "My site's done! Check it out → http://localhost:3000". What do they see?

Localhost means "this computer" on every computer. You texted them the word "home" — they went to their house, not yours.

2. Why build on localhost first instead of editing the live site directly?

Users never touch your localhost — that's the point. It's the locked test kitchen: experiment freely, then deploy when it's right.

3. You deployed yesterday. Today Claude updates your headline — localhost:3000 shows the new one, but yoursite.com still shows the old. What's going on?

Live is a snapshot from your last deploy; localhost is the living draft. Ship the new version and the live site catches up — you saw this exact beat in the widget.
