Imagine one headquarters answering every phone call for the whole planet. Tokyo calls — the phone rings in Virginia. São Paulo calls — same desk. Every caller waits while their question crosses an ocean, twice.

Now open a tiny desk in every city, staffed to answer the common questions. Suddenly almost every call is local. That's the flip you just made in the widget: same function, same code — the only thing that changed is where it ran. Tokyo went from ~290ms to ~20ms without anyone's internet getting faster.

Edge means running your code in small data centers near your users, instead of in one home base.

Why distance is lag

Data moves fast, but not instantly — and a request has to get there and back. Tokyo to Virginia is about 6,700 miles each way, plus stops at every relay in between. Do that round trip for a login check, then again for a redirect, then again for the page itself, and your app "feels slow" in half the world. Even after DNS points a visitor to the right address quickly, the miles still cost. The dot crawling across the track in origin mode? That's your user, waiting.

And 300 milliseconds sounds like nothing until you remember it happens on every tap. Five round trips to load one page is a second and a half of staring at nothing. Users don't file bug reports about that — they just quietly leave.

The CDN's sibling — but for code

You may know this trick from the CDN lesson: keep copies of files — images, video, the site itself — close to every visitor. The edge takes the next step. Instead of a saved copy of something, it's a live function executing in the node down the street: it can check things, decide things, and answer differently for every single visitor. A CDN hands out photocopies. The edge does actual work.

What belongs at the edge

Small, fast decisions that happen on every visit:

  • Auth checks — "is this person logged in?" answered next door, not across an ocean
  • Redirects — send Japanese visitors to /jp the instant they arrive
  • Personalization — swap the headline, currency, or banner per visitor
  • A/B splits — coin-flip visitors into test groups with zero added lag

There are limits. Edge nodes are small, built for tiny, fast functions — the serverless idea, just deployed to hundreds of locations instead of one. Your big database usually still lives back at the origin. So heavy lifting — reports, payments, anything that needs the full records — still happens at headquarters. The edge handles the greeting at the door; the origin still runs the kitchen.

Pro tip: keep edge code self-contained. If your "nearby" function has to phone the origin database five times to answer, you've rebuilt the long-distance call with extra steps. The win only exists when the edge can answer from what it has on hand.

This is a big part of why modern apps feel instant: the common questions moved next door, and only the rare, heavy ones still travel. When you deploy on platforms like Vercel or Cloudflare, your AI-built app often gets this for free — Claude writes a small middleware function, the platform quietly runs it in 300 cities, and nobody in Tokyo ever sees the spinner.

Stop making Tokyo call Virginia just to ask if someone's logged in.

✓ Check yourself

1. Users in Sydney say your checkout "feels laggy." Your server is in Virginia, and the slow part is a quick logged-in check on every click. What's the real fix?

A faster Virginia server still makes Sydney cross the Pacific twice per click. Moving the check next door is what took Tokyo from ~290ms to ~20ms in the widget.

2. Your site already uses a CDN. What does adding edge functions give you that the CDN doesn't?

CDN = photocopies of your files, parked nearby. Edge = your function executing nearby. One serves; the other thinks.

3. Which of these tasks belongs at the edge?

Edge nodes are for small, instant decisions. Heavy jobs that need the full database stay at the origin — the big records don't live at the edge.
