Lunch rush at a diner. Thirty orders hit the kitchen in ten minutes. The kitchen does not grow thirty arms — it has a rail. That steel strip above the pass where paper tickets clip on, in order, and the cook works through them one at a time.

Nobody's burger gets forgotten. Nobody's order collides with anyone else's. The rush doesn't break the kitchen; it just makes the rail longer for a while.

That's a queue: a waiting line for work. New tasks clip on at the back, get handled one at a time from the front, and nothing gets dropped.

Spikes become lines, not crashes

You just ran the experiment. With the rail plugged in, rush hour was boring — eight orders landed, the rail grew, the cook kept the exact same steady pace, and the line melted back down. Then you unplugged the rail, hit rush hour again, and watched orders bounce off a busy cook: dropped ❌.

That's the whole pitch. Your server can only do so much at once. When a viral moment or a Black Friday dumps ten thousand requests on it in a minute, a queue turns a deadly spike into a long, survivable line. Work arrives faster than it can be done — and that's fine, because arriving and being done are now two separate events. The order matters too: first in, first out. Engineers say FIFO. The diner just says "the rail."

The register never waits for the kitchen

The queue's second superpower is decoupling. The part of your app that takes orders — the register — responds instantly: "Got it, ticket #84." It doesn't wait for the kitchen, because it never promised the food now; it promised the food eventually. That's why a checkout page confirms in half a second even though fulfillment takes a day, and why "send to 10,000 subscribers" returns immediately while the emails trickle out over an hour.

Front and back run at their own speeds, with the rail as the buffer between them. It's the same start-now-finish-later instinct you'll see in async.

What "your job is queued" actually means

When your AI video tool says "queued — position 14," nothing is wrong. Your job is a ticket on the rail with thirteen tickets ahead of it, and a pool of workers is eating through the line. The worst thing you can do is resubmit — that just clips more tickets onto the rail. Queued means: received, safe, and scheduled. It's the most reassuring word in software, once you know what it means.

Warning sign: a healthy queue breathes — longer during spikes, shorter after. A queue that only ever grows means the kitchen can't keep up, and no rail is long enough to fix that. The answer isn't a bigger queue. It's more cooks.
