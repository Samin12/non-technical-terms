YAGNI

TLDRDon't build what you don't need yet.

Play it twice: once saying yes to everything, once saying no. Watch what happens to the ship date — and to the users.

You're packing for a weekend trip. Two nights. But what if it snows? What if you get invited to a gala? What if you take up scuba diving mid-trip? Pack for every possibility and you'll need four suitcases — and you'll miss the flight.

That's the game you just played. Every feature offer sounded reasonable in the moment. Each one cost three weeks. Accept enough of them and the ship date stops being a date.

YAGNI — "You Aren't Gonna Need It" — means don't build features for an imagined future. Build what's needed now, ship it, and let reality tell you what's next.

You're terrible at predicting the future. So is everyone.

The features you accepted weren't dumb. Dark mode is nice. Team mode could be big someday. That's exactly what makes feature creep dangerous — it never feels like creep. It feels like vision.

But the track record is brutal: study after study of real products finds that a large share of built features go rarely or never used. Builders guess wrong constantly — not because they're bad at their jobs, but because guessing is the wrong tool. The only reliable source of "what to build next" is users actually using the thing. And users can't use a thing that hasn't shipped.

The game's best ending says it plainly: ship the small version, and your users come back asking for exactly one feature. Now you're not guessing. You know.

Unused features aren't free — they're tenants

Those maintenance chips you collected are the hidden half of the lesson. A feature isn't a one-time cost. From the day it ships, it has to be tested, secured, debugged, and kept compatible with everything around it — forever. The blockchain sync nobody uses still breaks when you upgrade something else, and you still have to fix it.

Speculative features are also the fastest on-ramp to technical debt: you take on all the ongoing cost without ever collecting the value. Every "what if" you skip is code that can never break, confuse Claude, or slow down the next change.

AI made building cheap. That moves the discipline.

Here's the modern twist. With Claude, adding dark mode might genuinely take twenty minutes. So why not accept everything?

Because building was never the expensive part — owning is. Everything your AI adds is something you maintain, explain, and debug for the life of the product. When building gets cheap, the bottleneck moves to choosing. The scarce skill in the vibe coding era isn't "can you make it?" It's "should this exist yet?"

The YAGNI prompt, word for word: end your build requests to Claude with "Build ONLY this. Nothing speculative — no extra settings, no just-in-case options, no features I didn't ask for." The output gets faster, smaller, and easier to check. It pairs perfectly with KISS: the simple version, of only the needed thing.

What this unlocks for you

  • Shipping in weeks instead of quarters — momentum is a feature
  • Letting real users vote on feature #2 with their behavior, not your hunch
  • Less code overall, which means fewer bugs, less debt, cheaper changes
  • A polite, confident "not yet" for every shiny idea — including your own

To be clear, YAGNI isn't anti-ambition. It's a sequencing rule. The 17 languages can absolutely happen — the day someone in Reykjavik actually signs up. Until then, the suitcase stays light and the flight gets caught.

The cheapest feature to maintain is the one you didn't build yet.
