DNS

TLDRThe internet's phone book.

Open your phone and call your mom. You tapped "Mom." You did not dial +1-555-867-5309 from memory — you don't know that number. Your contacts app knows it, and it dialed for you.

Here's the thing: you pull the same trick hundreds of times a day on the internet without realizing. Every site you visit lives on a server, and every server's real address is a number — an IP address like 76.76.21.21. You never type those. You type names.

DNS is the internet's phone book: it turns the names humans remember into the numbers machines actually dial.

Names are for you. Numbers are for machines.

That's the entire division of labor. claudeclub.com is for your brain; 76.76.21.21 is for your browser. The lookup chain you just watched runs every time you visit somewhere new: your browser asks "who is this name?", a DNS resolver checks the phone book, the number comes back, and only then does the site load — from the number, never from the name. The whole dance takes a few dozen milliseconds.

And you felt the alternative. The "world without DNS" mode — squinting at digits, typing them exactly, getting one wrong and reaching a stranger's server — that's the job DNS quietly does for you on every single click. Forever.

One more thing you may have noticed: look the same name up twice, and the second answer comes back in about a millisecond. Your computer keeps recent answers instead of asking again — that's a cache, and it matters in a moment.

"Point your domain": what launching a site really means

When you deploy a site — say, the one Claude just built with you — it lands on a server with some IP address. When you buy mybakery.com, you've bought a name: an empty contact card. "Changing your DNS" or "pointing your domain" just means writing the entry — mybakery.com → 84.19.7.220. That one line in the phone book is what connects the name people type to the machine you shipped to. Move to a better host next year? Update the entry. The name follows you, and visitors never notice a thing.

Why DNS changes take time to spread

There isn't one phone book. There are thousands of copies cached all over the planet — your laptop keeps one, your wifi router keeps one, your internet provider keeps a big one. When you update your entry, the copies don't all refresh instantly; each waits for its stored answer to expire before asking again. That's why a brand-new domain can work on your laptop while a friend across the ocean gets nothing for a few hours. Registrars call it "propagation." Nothing is broken. It's just a lot of phone books updating on their own schedules.

Launch-day pro tip: if your new domain works for you but not for a friend, do not panic-redeploy. Nine times out of ten your site is fine and their phone book is simply stale. Give it a few hours before you touch anything.

What this unlocks for you

  • Buy a domain and point it at anything you've deployed — site, store, app
  • Switch hosts without changing your name or losing a single visitor
  • Decode registrar emails: "propagation" is just phone books syncing
  • Diagnose "works for me, not for them" like someone who's seen it before
