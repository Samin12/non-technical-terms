You don't have to cook to eat at a great restaurant. You describe what you want, the chef makes it, you taste it, and you send it back if it's not right. Nobody hands you a knife and says "first, learn to julienne."

For seventy years, software didn't work like that. If you wanted an app, you learned to cook — syntax, semicolons, stack traces — or you hired someone who had. That wall just fell.

Vibe coding is building software by describing what you want in plain English and letting AI write the code — then steering with feedback instead of syntax.

You just did it. The pomodoro timer in the widget is a real, running app. You ordered it, the kitchen built it, and when you wanted a break button and bigger numbers, you didn't open the code. You said so.

The loop: describe → generate → refine

That three-beat loop is the entire craft. Describe what you want. Let Claude generate it. Then look at the running thing and refine: "the button's too small," "it should pause when I switch tabs," "make it orange." Each refinement is a new order to the kitchen — and you taste between every course.

In real life this loop runs inside tools like Claude Code, where an agent writes the files, runs the app, reads the errors, and fixes them — the widget you just used, scaled up to anything.

You can only refine what you can name

Here's the part nobody puts on the poster: the chef is only as good as your order. "Make it better" gets you a shrug in code form. "The numbers reset when I refresh — keep the state" gets you a fix in one pass. Same app, same AI. The difference is vocabulary.

That's not a coding skill. It's a naming skill — and it's the entire reason this site exists. Every lesson on this path is another word you can use in an order: deploy it, the API call is failing, cache that, try it on a branch first. The vocabulary is the steering wheel. Without it you're a passenger; with it, vibe coding turns you into a builder who happens not to type the code.

Where it breaks — and what fixes it

The AI will occasionally serve a beautiful dish that's raw inside: code that looks right and quietly isn't. That's hallucination, and it doesn't go away because you asked nicely. So the non-negotiable rule: run it, click it, try to break it. You are the taster. The describing is fast; the tasting is the job.

And a toy on your laptop isn't a product. When something works, you save the progress with version control — in the widget, rebuilding from the original prompt wiped your refinements; real builders never lose work that way — and you push it live with a deploy. Both are lessons on this path.

Never ship what you haven't personally clicked through. Vibe coding doesn't remove the work — it moves the work from writing code to verifying it. That trade is fantastic. It is not optional.

The people winning with AI right now aren't the best typists of code. They're the clearest describers of problems — the ones who can name what they see.

You bring the taste and the vocabulary. The AI brings the syntax. That trade is the whole revolution.

✓ Check yourself

1. Your vibe-coded invoice tool looks perfect, and the AI says it's done. Before you send a real client invoice with it, what's the must-do step?

Vibe coding moves your job from writing to verifying. Confident-but-wrong output is a known failure mode — you are the taster before anything ships.

2. Two builders refine the same buggy timer. A says "make it better." B says "the timer resets on refresh — keep the state." Why does B get a fix in one pass?

You can only refine what you can name. "State," "refresh," "reset" — each word aims the fix. That vocabulary is exactly what this site is teaching you.

3. Your vibe-coded app runs great on your laptop, and friends want to use it. What's the next move called?

An app on your laptop is visible to exactly one person. Deploying puts it on a server the world can reach — that's a later lesson on this path.
