Voice Agents

TLDRAn agent you just talk to.

Calling most phone menus feels like talking to a filing cabinet. "Press 2 for billing." Now imagine the voice on the line actually listens, checks the calendar while you're still mid-sentence, books the thing, and answers like a person who was paying attention.

That's a voice agent: a phone call with an assistant who can actually do things while you talk.

A voice agent is an agent with ears and a mouth bolted on: your speech becomes text, an agent thinks and acts on it, and the reply becomes speech again.

The three-stage relay

You just ran the relay. Stage one, the ears: speech-to-text turns your sound waves into a transcript. Stage two, the brain: a real agent — the same think-act loop from the Agent lesson — reads the transcript and gets to work, often calling an API or two mid-call to check availability or book the table. Stage three, the mouth: text-to-speech turns the written reply back into a voice.

Each stage costs milliseconds, and they stack — you watched the counters add up to one total. That total is everything, because it's the silence the caller sits through.

Why three separate machines instead of one? Because each stage is its own specialist: one model is great at hearing, one at thinking, one at speaking. Newer "speech-to-speech" systems are starting to merge the relay into a single model to shave off the handoffs — but the mental model, and the latency math, stay exactly the same.

Latency is the whole game

Humans take turns in conversation fast — usually a few hundred milliseconds. Anything much past 800 ms of silence and your brain quietly decides the line is dead. You felt it in the widget: at 2,000 ms of thinking time, that "…" bubble stops being a pause and becomes a problem. The reply can be flawless; if it's late, the call feels broken.

So builders fight for milliseconds with three classic tricks:

  • Streaming: start speaking the first tokens of the reply while the rest is still being written
  • Filler speech: "one sec — checking that for you…" buys the agent a full second of working time without dead air
  • Barge-in: the instant you interrupt, a good agent stops talking and re-plans — just like a human would
Pro tip: when you test any voice agent, interrupt it mid-sentence. Cheap demos bulldoze on and finish their paragraph. Real products handle barge-in gracefully — it's the fastest quality tell there is, the voice-agent version of a firm handshake.

Where voice agents shine

Text agents wait for you to sit down at a keyboard. Voice meets you where you already are:

  • Phone lines — bookings, support, an after-hours receptionist that actually resolves things
  • Cars and kitchens — anywhere your hands and eyes are busy
  • Field work — technicians updating tickets by voice between jobs
  • Accessibility — people who can't comfortably type get the same AI leverage as everyone else

One more thing to notice: the conversation itself is still a session — the agent has to remember you said "for two" when you later say "actually, make it 8." And every word of the transcript costs context, just like a text chat: long calls fill the window the same way long conversations do. Same rules as text, just at the speed of speech.

The magic isn't that it talks. It's that it works while it talks — fast enough that you never notice the relay.

✓ Check yourself

1. A voice agent gives perfect answers, but callers keep saying "hello? you there?" before it replies. What's the actual problem?

You felt this on the slider. In voice, the pause IS the product — a flawless answer that arrives late still feels like a broken call.

2. Mid-call, the agent says "One sec — checking availability…" before answering. What is that, really?

That sentence buys a full second of agent work with zero dead air. It's not politeness — it's engineering wearing politeness as a disguise.

3. You interrupt the agent mid-reply: "actually, make it 8pm." What should a well-built voice agent do?

Humans yield the floor instantly when interrupted. Barge-in is a voice agent doing the same — and it's the clearest tell of a well-built one.
