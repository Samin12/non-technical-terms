An IKEA wardrobe and a pile of raw lumber can become the same piece of furniture. One takes an afternoon and an Allen key. The other takes a workshop, power tools, and a few weekends of expensive mistakes.

Building an AI agent used to be the lumber version. You'd hand-build the loop, the retries, the tool wiring, the safety stops — months of plumbing before your agent did anything useful. Most people never got past the sawdust.

An Agents SDK is a starter kit for building your own AI worker: the loop, the plumbing, and the safety screws come pre-drilled — you just choose the parts.

What's in the box

The flat-packed parts you get for free:

  • The agent loop — plan, act, check, repeat — the engine of every agent, already assembled and debugged
  • Tool plumbing — a standard socket for handing the model tools and routing results back; it's also where MCP connectors plug in
  • Context management — long sessions get trimmed automatically so the agent doesn't lose the plot mid-task
  • Retries and seatbelts — failed steps retry, dangerous steps pause for permission

Notice something? That list is a harness. An Agents SDK is a harness in kit form — the whole car, flat-packed, with the engine mount already welded. Under the hood it talks to the model through the API, but you never touch that wiring.

What you bring

Your job is the three decisions from the widget. Which model — the engine. Which tools — the hands. What instructions — the job description. And the tools matter more than people expect: you watched the inbox agent improvise a to-do list when you withheld the email sender. It didn't fail loudly or fake the send. It worked with what it had — because an agent's real capabilities are exactly its tool list, nothing more.

This is the part no SDK can do for you: knowing what's worth automating, which tools the job truly needs, and what "done" looks like. The kit handles the engineering. You supply the judgment.

That ratio is the unlock. The plumbing used to be 80% of the project and 100% of the reason people quit. Now it ships in the box, and your afternoon goes into the 20% that was always the actual product.

Start embarrassingly small. Two tools, one narrow goal, supervised runs. Every tool you add is a new way for your agent to wander off-script — capability and chaos rise together. The best first agent is the smallest one that's actually useful.

✓ Check yourself

1. You're building a research agent with an Agents SDK. Which part do you NOT have to build yourself?

The SDK's whole pitch: loop, plumbing, retries, and seatbelts come pre-built. You bring the parts list and the job description.

2. In the widget, you gave the inbox agent no email sender — but the goal needed email. What happened, and what's the lesson?

No tool, no action — a well-built agent adapts and tells you. Tools aren't accessories; they are the agent's entire reach into the world.

3. Your assembled agent keeps doing weird extra stuff during runs. Best first fix?

Capability and chaos rise together. Shrinking the toolset and sharpening the goal is the boring fix that almost always works first.
