Picture your first hire. Brilliant, fast, never sleeps — but with one ridiculous rule: before answering any question, they must re-read the entire company handbook. Front to back. Every time.

"What's our refund policy?" Three hundred pages, then an answer. "Do we ship to Canada?" Three hundred pages. Again. You're not paying for answers anymore. You're paying for the same reading, over and over.

Prompt caching fixes that: the AI reads the big, repeated part of your prompt once, then reuses that reading for a fraction of the price.

Why your AI re-reads everything

Here's the part nobody tells you: the model has no memory between requests. None. Every time you call it, you send the entire pile again — the system prompt, your docs, your examples, the whole conversation so far. The model reads every token of it, and you're billed for every token it reads.

So a support bot carrying a 50,000-token product manual doesn't pay for that manual once. It pays on question one, question two, question two hundred. Same manual, same reading, fresh bill. That's the 🐢 button you kept pressing in the race above — $0.15 and 4.2 seconds, every single time.

What the cache actually does

With caching on, the provider keeps a snapshot of the heavy reading work — the already-processed version of your prompt's opening section. The next request checks: does this prompt start with the exact same 50,000 tokens? It does? Skip straight to the new part.

That's the ⚡ button: same question, same answer, roughly 10× cheaper and seconds faster, because the handbook was already in memory. The employee read it this morning. Now they just answer.

The first read isn't free, of course. Writing to the cache actually costs a little more than a normal read — you're paying the librarian to file everything properly. It pays for itself by the second question, which is why the race wasn't even close.

One catch: the snapshot doesn't live forever. Caches expire after a few minutes of silence. They're built for bursts of work — which is exactly what a busy chatbot session or an agent loop looks like.

The one rule: stable stuff first

The cache only matches from the very beginning of the prompt. Change one early word and the snapshot is worthless — full re-read, full price.

So builders arrange every prompt the same way: stable content first (system prompt, docs, examples), changing content last (the user's question, today's data). And remember — caching makes the desk cheaper to refill, it doesn't make the desk bigger. Everything still has to fit in the context window.

The classic cache-killer: putting today's date, a timestamp, or the user's name at the top of the prompt. Now every request starts differently, the cache never matches, and you silently pay cold price forever. Anything that changes goes at the end.

2. A builder puts "Today is June 11" at the very top of the prompt, before 50,000 tokens of docs. Why is the bill still at full cold price?

The cache matches from the first token onward. One changing word up top makes every request look brand new. Anything that changes belongs at the end.

3. Which workload gets the biggest win from prompt caching?

Caching pays off when a big chunk of the prompt repeats. Agents are the extreme case — same handbook, dozens of reads per task.
