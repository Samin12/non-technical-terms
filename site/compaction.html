Compaction

TLDRSummarizing the chat so it keeps fitting.

Think about the last good meeting you were in. Two hours of talk — and what survived? Nobody keeps the transcript. Someone writes notes: what we decided, who owns what, what's next. The transcript was the meeting. The notes are what the meeting becomes.

Compaction is your AI doing exactly that to your own conversation: summarizing the older messages into a short recap so the chat keeps fitting in its limited memory.

Why it has to happen

Your AI's working memory — the context window — is a fixed budget of tokens. Every message you send and every reply it writes piles onto the same desk. A long session will hit the ceiling. Guaranteed. It's arithmetic, not bad luck.

At the ceiling there are exactly two options. Option one: the oldest messages silently fall off the desk and are simply gone. Option two: squeeze the old stuff into a summary first — keep the gist, free the space. Compaction is option two, and it's what Claude does automatically when a conversation gets near the limit. That's the pulsing button you just pressed in the widget: fifteen-odd messages of build talk collapsed into one summary chip, the meter dropping from red back to nearly empty, and the conversation just… kept going.

And no, compaction is not the same as starting over. A fresh session wipes the desk completely — perfect between unrelated tasks. Compaction is for when you're mid-project and the history still matters: it keeps a compressed thread of everything agreed so far, so you don't have to re-negotiate it from scratch.

What survives the squeeze — and what doesn't

A good summary keeps decisions and current state: orange theme, login bug fixed, Stripe for payments, app renamed Brewlog. That's why work can continue after a compact as if nothing happened.

But summaries are lossy. By design. The exact hex code you mentioned once, the reasoning behind rejecting an option, that offhand requirement from message #3 — any of those can get smoothed away. Just like meeting notes: "we picked Stripe" survives; the twenty minutes of why not the alternative usually doesn't.

After a compact, never assume every detail made it through. If something is critical — an exact spec, a hard rule, a number — re-state it right after the squeeze. Better yet, keep must-not-lose facts in a file like CLAUDE.md that gets re-read fresh, outside the conversation entirely. Summaries trade detail for room. Know which details you can't afford to trade.

How to work with it

  • Compact at milestones, not in a panic. In Claude Code you can trigger it yourself with /compact. Doing it right after finishing a feature — a natural chapter break — produces a far better summary than an automatic squeeze mid-task.
  • Watch the meter. Past roughly 80% full, answer quality starts slipping before any compaction happens. The widget turned red for a reason.
  • Re-anchor after. One message — "quick recap: we're doing X, constraint Y still applies" — re-plants anything fragile.

What this unlocks for you

  • All-day working sessions that don't die when the window fills
  • An explanation for late-session amnesia: the AI is reading its own meeting notes now, not the transcript
  • Control — compact on purpose at clean break points instead of at the worst possible moment
  • A tighter, cheaper conversation: a 3,000-token recap beats 14,000 tokens of scroll-back

Keep the decisions, drop the transcript — that's compaction, and it's why a good conversation can run all day.

✓ Check yourself

1. Three hours into a session, you see "compacting conversation…". What is actually happening?

Compaction trades the old transcript for a summary, so the session keeps fitting in the context window without starting over.

2. After a compact, Claude forgets the exact hex code you gave it an hour ago — but still knows you're building a dark theme. Why?

That's the lossy trade: "dark theme" is a decision, six hex digits are a detail. Re-state critical specifics after a squeeze — or keep them in a file.

3. Your context meter reads 85% and you have hours of work ahead. The smart move?

A deliberate compact at a milestone produces a clean summary. Waiting for the emergency squeeze — or pasting more in — only makes the desk worse.
