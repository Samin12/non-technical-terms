Git & Version Control

TLDRSave points for your project.

You know save points from video games. Right before the boss door, you hit save. Then you try the fight a hundred different ways — rush in swinging, sneak around the back, test the weird fire sword — because no matter how badly it goes, you respawn at the door and go again.

Now imagine the thing you're building — your site, your app, your content system — had that exact mechanic. It does. It's called Git.

Git is a save system for your project: every version you save is kept forever, and you can restore any of them in seconds.

It's a small program that lives on your computer and watches one folder. No cloud, no internet required, and it doesn't care what's in the folder — code, essays, cookie recipes. When you say "save point," it photographs everything and files it away. Nearly every piece of software you've ever used was built on top of this one tool.

Why Cmd+Z was never enough

Undo walks backward one keystroke at a time, and it dies the moment you close the file. So people invent folders: final, final-v2, FINAL-real-this-time. That's version control by hand, and it collapses the first time you need to know which "final" actually worked. Git replaces all of it with one clean idea: one project folder, with a labeled timeline of save points behind it. Each save point remembers the entire project, what changed, and when.

You just felt the magic

In the widget above, you edited the poem, dropped a save point, kept editing, then hit Disaster and watched the whole thing mangle. Then — one click on the timeline — every word came back. No panic, no archaeology, no "wait, what did line two say?" That calm is the entire product. Each save point you dropped is called a commit, and it gets its own lesson next.

Notice the counter nagging you when unsaved edits piled up. Real builders save constantly — small save points, made often. Git can also run parallel timelines called branches, so you experiment on a copy while the original stays safe. And when you want your timeline backed up and shared with other people, that's what GitHub is for.

Make this your first habit: save before you let the AI loose. One commit before every big Claude request. If the AI rewrites half your project and you hate it, you restore in one click and try a different prompt — same boss fight, new strategy. This single habit is what makes vibe coding safe instead of scary.

What this unlocks for you

Once save points exist, a whole way of working opens up:

  • Fearless experiments — try the scary change; the worst case is a one-click restore
  • A safety net under AI — let Claude refactor boldly, knowing nothing is ever truly lost
  • Time travel for debugging — walk back through saves to find the exact moment something broke
  • The foundation of teamwork — branches, pull requests, GitHub: all of it stands on these save points

Builders aren't braver than you. They've just always got a save point waiting outside the boss door.

Save early, save often, fear nothing.

✓ Check yourself

1. You're about to ask Claude to redesign your entire homepage. What's the smart move first?

Commit before the boss fight. If the AI's version is worse, restore and re-prompt. Undo can't rescue you across many files and sessions — save points can.

2. You made a dozen quick edits, everything broke, and you restore your last save point. What do you get?

A restore is exact: the whole project rewinds to that photograph. That guarantee is what makes disasters boring instead of devastating.

3. How is Git different from keeping folders like website-final-v2-REAL?

No duplicate folders, no guessing which "final" is final. The whole history lives behind one folder, labeled and instantly restorable.
