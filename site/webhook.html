Imagine expecting a package and checking your front door every thirty seconds. Open the door. Nothing. Close it. Wait. Open it again. Nothing. You'd do it hundreds of times for one delivery — which is exactly what the left panel just did, and exactly why its wasted-trips counter looks embarrassing.

The sane move is to install a doorbell and let the courier press it. One ring, the moment it matters, zero wasted trips. That's the right panel.

A webhook is the doorbell: instead of you asking an app "anything new?", the app pings your address the instant something happens.

Pull vs. push

A normal API call is pull: you ask, it answers. Great when you want something on demand — but a terrible way to find out about events. Poll every two seconds and you hammer the API thousands of times a day for mostly "nothing yet…". Poll gently, every ten minutes, and your "instant" payment alert is nine minutes stale. Pull makes you choose between wasteful and slow.

A webhook is push. You hand the other app an address, and it calls you — once per event, zero delay, nothing wasted. In the widget, every payment rang the bell in the same instant you clicked it. The polling side caught the same payments, but always late, and buried under empty checks.

The doorbell address

Here's the twist that makes webhooks click: the address you hand over is an endpoint on your side. The roles flip. You're not the one knocking on Stripe's doors anymore — you've built a door of your own, something like yoursite.com/hooks/stripe, and told Stripe "when a payment succeeds, send the details here." The ping itself is just a small request carrying a payload, like the one you watched arrive: event: payment.succeeded · amount: $49.

You'll meet these everywhere: Stripe pinging you on every payment, a form tool pinging when someone submits, GitHub pinging when code is pushed — which is the classic trigger for an automatic deploy. And note the difference from a cron job: cron fires on a clock, a webhook fires on an event. "Every morning at 9" is cron. "The second someone pays" is a webhook.

When an automation silently stops working, check the doorbell first. Webhooks fail quietly: if your receiving address changed or your server was napping, the sender shrugs, maybe retries a few times, then gives up — and nobody emails you about it. Moved your site? Re-point every webhook. And treat the URL itself like it's semi-secret: anyone who knows your doorbell address can ring it.

What this unlocks for you

Once you can receive pings, your tools start talking to you instead of waiting to be asked:

  • "Someone paid" → a Slack message and a thank-you email, within a second
  • Form submission → contact filed into your CRM, no copy-paste
  • Code pushed → site rebuilds and ships itself
  • Agents that wake up on events — your AI springs into action because something happened, not because a timer went off

Look at any automation platform — Zapier, n8n, all of them — and squint: almost every "trigger" is a webhook and almost every "action" is an API call. Your whole automation stack is doorbells glued to drive-thru windows.

APIs are how your software asks. Webhooks are how it gets told.

✓ Check yourself

1. You want a Slack message the moment anyone buys your product. The cleanest setup?

"The moment it happens" is push territory. Polling every minute is wasteful AND up to a minute late; the webhook rings once, instantly, only when there's news.

2. What actually is the webhook URL you paste into Stripe's settings?

It's your doorbell: an endpoint you control. Stripe pushes a small payload (event name, amount, ID) to that address every time the event fires.

3. Your "new sale → email the customer" automation worked for months, then silently stopped right after you moved your site to a new host. Most likely cause?

Webhooks fail silently: Stripe kept ringing an address that no longer answers, then gave up. After any move, re-point your webhooks first.
