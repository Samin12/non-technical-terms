Every single move costs a full look-think-act cycle. That's why computer use is slower than an API — and why it works on apps an API can't touch.

Most well-run apps have a staff entrance around the back — the API — where software walks in, places an order, and leaves. But a huge amount of the world's software has no staff entrance at all. The only way in is the front door: the screen, the mouse, the keyboard.

Computer use is your AI walking in the front door. No special access, no integration, no permission slip from the app's developers. It uses the exact same interface you do.

Computer use is an AI operating a computer the way a person does: looking at the screen, moving the cursor, clicking buttons, and typing into boxes.

Look, think, act, repeat

You just watched the whole trick. The ghost cursor wasn't following a script — in a real run, Claude literally takes a screenshot (LOOK), decides on one small action (THINK), performs it (MOVE, CLICK, TYPE), then screenshots again to check what happened. It has no magical awareness of the screen. It re-looks constantly, like driving at night: headlights show a little road, you move, you look again.

That loop is what makes this an agent behavior, not a chat trick. The model isn't describing how one would log an expense. It's actually doing it — one verified step at a time, hands on your mouse. And because every step gets checked with fresh eyes, it can recover from surprises — a cookie banner, a login prompt — the same way you would: look again, adjust, continue.

When the front door is the only door

If an app has an API, use the API. It's the staff entrance: faster, cheaper, far sturdier. Computer use is for everything else:

  • That accounting program from 2009 your business still runs on
  • Government portals and internal tools that will never ship an API
  • Websites that only exist as forms and buttons
  • Testing your own app the way a real human user experiences it

It's also slower and more fragile, and you should know why. Each tiny action costs a full look-think-act cycle — your $40 expense took thirteen steps and three screenshots. Screenshots are heavy to process. And because it navigates by what it sees, a redesigned button or a surprise popup can send it somewhere you didn't intend. An API call is one order at the window; computer use is a whole errand. Often it runs in a headless browser — same clicking, no visible screen, nobody watching.

Safety first: an AI with computer use can click anything you can click — including "Send," "Pay," and "Delete." Supervise early runs end to end, use a separate browser profile or sandbox account, and keep it away from anything irreversible. Treat it like a brilliant new intern at your keyboard: fast, capable, and not yet trusted with the company card.

What this unlocks for you

  • Automate the un-automatable — software with no API gets the same AI leverage as everything else
  • Fill the same form 200 times without losing your mind (or your afternoon)
  • QA-test your product by having an AI use it like a confused first-time customer
  • Bridge the gap while you wait for a proper API or MCP connection to exist

The pattern to remember: staff entrance when it exists, front door when it doesn't. Between the two, almost nothing on a screen is out of reach anymore.

APIs are the staff entrance. Computer use is the front door — and your AI just learned to open it.
