API

TLDRHow your AI talks to other apps.

Try it — the API drive-thru

You're the AI. The window below is another app's API. Order something off the menu — but you'll need your key first.

The window is open. Place an order…

Notice: no key → rejected. Not on the menu → rejected. That's the entire security model of the modern internet.

If you've ever used a drive-thru, you already understand APIs.

Your AI doesn't have hands. It can't log into your apps, click around, or fill out forms. It doesn't need to. Shopify has an API. Slack, Stripe, Gmail, Notion — almost every app you use has one. The API is how your AI actually gets things done in the real world.

So picture the drive-thru.

Your AI pulls up to the window. There's a menu bolted to the wall: fetch these orders, send this email, post this message, update this product. It reads the menu, places an order, and the kitchen does the work. Your AI never walks inside. It never sees the kitchen. It just orders off what's posted, and the food comes out.

That's an API: the window between your AI and somebody else's software.

The menu is the whole point — and the safety

The only things your AI can do are the things printed on that menu. If "delete every customer" isn't on the menu, it literally cannot be ordered. You just saw this in the simulator: the window doesn't take requests that aren't listed.

So an API isn't just a way in. It's a fence. The app decides exactly what's on offer, and everything else stays locked in the back where nobody at the window can reach it. That's why companies are comfortable opening a window at all — they're not handing you keys to the kitchen, they're handing you a menu they wrote.

Every window needs a key

To order, your AI has to prove it's allowed at the window. That proof is the API key: a long random string that says "this order comes from my account — let it through, bill it to me." No key, no service. You saw that too: the 401 rejection until you attached the key.

Never paste your API key directly into your code. Code ends up on GitHub, much of GitHub is public, and bots scan it for leaked keys around the clock. A leaked key is your credit card taped to the drive-thru window. Keys live in a separate hidden file — that's the Env File lesson, coming up.

What this unlocks for you

Once you see the window, "how does AI actually do stuff" clicks into place. With APIs, your AI can:

  • Pull last week's orders out of Shopify and summarize them
  • Add a new subscriber to your email list the second they sign up
  • Drop a message in Slack when something breaks
  • Create calendar events, send emails, update prices — while you sleep

None of that is the AI being clever with words. It's the AI placing orders at windows that were already open. Without APIs, you've got a really good talker — it can write a beautiful email and then sit there, unable to send it. With APIs, it walks up to the window, orders "send this," and it's gone.

Without APIs you've got a chatbot. With them, you've got an employee.

✓ Check yourself

1. Your AI needs to add a row to your Notion database. What does it actually do?

AI doesn't have hands — it places orders at the window. Notion's API lists "add a row" on its menu, so your AI orders exactly that.

2. Why is an API also a safety fence?

The app's owner decides what's on the menu. If "delete every customer" isn't listed, no request — however clever — can make the kitchen cook it.

3. Where should your API key live?

Keys in code leak to GitHub, and bots find them in minutes. They belong in a hidden env file — full lesson on that in Level 5.
