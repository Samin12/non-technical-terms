Cron Job

TLDRAn alarm clock for tasks.

A coffee maker with a timer is a tiny miracle. You load it at night, set 7:00 AM, and go to bed. It doesn't ask if you're awake. It doesn't wait for permission. The clock hits 7:00 — it brews.

Computers have had this exact feature since the 1970s. It's called cron, and a cron job is one task you've put on the timer: a script, a backup, a report — anything — set to fire at the times you choose, forever.

A cron job is a task that runs itself on a schedule. You set the alarm once; the work happens without you.

Reading the five stars

The schedule language looks like someone fell asleep on the keyboard: 0 9 * * 1. But you just built these in the widget, so you already know the secret — it's five slots, read left to right: minute, hour, day of month, month, weekday. A star means "every." Hover the five cells in the builder and each slot tells you what it accepts.

So 0 9 * * 1 says: minute 0, hour 9, any day, any month, weekday 1 — every Monday at 9:00 AM. And the infamous * * * * *, every slot wide open, fires every single minute. 1,440 times a day. You watched it flood the week with ten thousand runs. Every builder makes that mistake exactly once for real — be the one who made it in a widget instead.

The machine has to be on

Cron isn't magic — it's a little daemon that wakes up every minute, checks the schedule, and fires whatever matches. Which means the alarm clock only rings if the machine is awake. A cron job on your laptop dies the moment you close the lid.

That's why real schedules live on a server — a computer that never closes — and run headless, because nothing about a 4 AM backup needs a screen. It's also why cloud schedulers exist: someone else's always-awake machine, running your timers. Laptop for building, server for running. This whole trio of lessons is really one sentence.

Cron + agent = an employee

Backups and reports are the classics. But here's the 2026 version: put an AI agent on the timer. Every evening at 9 PM, one of my agents reads the day's AI news and drafts content. Every morning, another one checks what shipped overnight and writes me a briefing. I didn't hire anyone. I wrote a schedule.

The pattern scales as far as you want: cron fires the trigger, the agent does the judgment work, and you read the output with your coffee. The schedule is the shift; the agent is the employee. And it fires whether or not you remember it exists — that's the entire value: consistency you don't have to supply.

Worth scheduling this week:

  • A nightly backup of anything you'd cry about losing
  • A Monday-morning report that writes itself before you wake up
  • An agent that drafts tomorrow's content every evening — mine do
  • A weekly "is anything broken?" check on your site
Warning: cron is dumb on purpose. It fires on schedule whether the last run finished, failed, or set something on fire — no retries, no memory, no apology. Silent failure is cron's specialty, so make every scheduled task log somewhere you'll actually look, or better, message you when it fails.
