Skill

TLDRA saved SOP your AI follows on demand.

Walk into any serious restaurant kitchen and you’ll find them: laminated recipe cards. The Tuesday cook and the Saturday cook plate the same carbonara, because neither one is improvising — they’re following the card.

That's what you'll run in the widget below. “Do the weekly report” with the skill loaded: four steps, executed in order, report delivered. Without it: four clarifying questions and zero output. Same AI, same request. The difference was the card.

A skill is a saved procedure — steps, examples, even scripts — that your AI loads and follows whenever a matching task shows up.

Try it — run the SOP

Here’s a saved skill — a recipe card for one recurring task. Ask for the report with the card in hand, then try the same request without it.

📋 weekly-report/SKILL.md — the laminated card
☐ 1. Fetch this week’s numbers
☐ 2. Build the chart
☐ 3. Write a 3-line summary
☐ 4. Email it to the team
Same four words either way: “do the weekly report.” The card makes the difference.

Run it both ways. With the card: four steps, done. Without it: your afternoon disappears into clarifying questions.

Write the recipe once

The first time you want Claude to do your weekly report, you’ll explain everything: which numbers, what kind of chart, how long the summary, who gets the email. That conversation is real work — so don’t throw it away. Capture it as a skill: a folder holding a SKILL.md file that describes the procedure, plus any examples or scripts the job needs.

Next Friday you say “do the weekly report” and the whole briefing is already there. The week after, same thing. The version of the morning where the AI asks “which numbers did you mean?” never happens again, because the answer is on the card.

On demand — not always-on

Here’s what separates a skill from house rules like CLAUDE.md. House rules apply to everything, all the time: never push to main, always use TypeScript. A skill is task-shaped, and it loads only when the task shows up.

Each skill carries a name and a short description of when to use it. When your request comes in, the AI scans its rack of cards, sees “weekly-report: build and email the Friday numbers,” and pulls that one. Ask about something unrelated, and the card stays on the rack — costing nothing, cluttering nothing. That’s why skills scale so well: you can keep fifty of them without slowing anything down, because only the relevant recipe gets read. You can also fire one explicitly with a slash command — that’s the next lesson.

Skills are how teams bottle judgment

A great skill holds more than steps. It carries your standards: the example of what a good summary looks like, the script that pulls the numbers the right way, the warning about the metric that always trips people up. That’s judgment, written down — and it’s what turns a generic agent into one that works the way you work.

Which means skills are shareable. Drop the folder in a shared repo, and your whole team’s AI now runs reports your way. The new hire’s agent cooks like your best operator on day one. Kitchens figured this out a century ago — the recipe book is the restaurant.

The moment you catch yourself explaining the same workflow to your AI for the second time — stop. That explanation is a skill begging to be saved. Ten minutes of writing it down buys you every future repetition for free.

What this unlocks for you

  • Output quality stops depending on how well you prompted today
  • Your best workflows survive — in files, not in your head

Stop being the only one who knows how it’s done. Laminate the card.
