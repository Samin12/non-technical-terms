Your AI doesn't have a memory the way you do. It has a desk.

Everything it knows about your conversation — your instructions, every message you've sent, every reply it gave, that 40-page PDF you pasted — all of it has to physically sit on that desk while it works. If something is on the desk, the AI can use it. If it's not on the desk, it does not exist. There is no filing cabinet behind it.

That desk is the context window. Its size is measured in tokens — the chunks AI reads text in. A model with a 200,000-token window has a desk that fits roughly 150,000 words. Sounds huge. Fills up faster than you think.

Why your AI "forgets"

You've felt this. Two hours into a long chat, the AI starts contradicting things you agreed on at the start. It's not being lazy — the desk overflowed. When new stuff arrives and there's no room, the oldest stuff gets pushed off the edge, exactly like the simulator above. The AI isn't ignoring your early instructions. From where it sits, they're simply gone.

What's actually taking up space

The desk holds more than your chat messages:

  • The standing instructions — the system prompt sits on the desk the entire time
  • Every file you attach — that PDF is thousands of tokens, permanently occupying desk space
  • The AI's own replies — yes, its answers count against the budget too
  • Tool results — when an agent reads files or searches the web, everything it finds lands on the desk
This is the single most useful debugging instinct for working with AI: when the answers get worse, ask "what's on the desk right now?" Usually the answer is: too much, and the important part fell off.

How builders work with it

You don't fight the desk. You manage it:

  • Start fresh sessions per task. A new session is a clean desk — that's why first answers feel so sharp.
  • Don't paste the whole manual when one page will do. Desk space is attention: the less clutter, the better the focus on what matters.
  • Summarize long chats. Tools like Claude do this automatically — it's called compaction, and it's the next-but-one lesson.

Once you start picturing the desk, half the "weird" AI behavior you've seen stops being weird. It's not moody. It's just out of desk.

✓ Check yourself

1. Two hours into a chat, the AI forgets a rule you set at the very start. What most likely happened?

Old messages get pushed out when the window fills. From the AI's view, your early rule doesn't exist anymore — it's not on the desk.

2. Which of these takes up context window space?

Everything the AI can "see" sits on the desk together. That's why a few big PDFs can crowd out an entire conversation.

3. Why does a brand-new session often feel smarter than a long-running one?

Same model, emptier desk. Less clutter competing for attention means sharper answers on the thing you just asked.
