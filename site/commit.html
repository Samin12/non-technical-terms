A commit is a labeled snapshot of your entire project at one moment in time.

Commits are the save points from the Git lesson — this is just their real name. Every commit you make becomes a node on the timeline you can return to, compare against, or build a branch from.

First, you choose what's in the frame

Notice you didn't have to photograph everything at once. The checkboxes were the staging step: out of all your recent edits, you pick the ones that belong together in this snapshot. Fixed a typo and redesigned the pricing page? That's two different stories — so it should be two commits. Small commits, each about one thing, are the mark of someone who knows what they're doing. Each one can be understood, shared, or undone on its own.

The hash is for computers. The label is for humans.

Every snapshot card got a code like a1b2c3d. That's the hash — a unique fingerprint machines use to point at that exact version. You'll almost never type it. The label is the part that matters to people. "Fix homepage typo" tells future-you, your teammate, and your AI exactly what happened here. "stuff" tells them nothing — which is why the widget gave you side-eye for it.

This isn't just politeness. When Claude works on your project, it reads the commit history to understand how the project evolved and why. Clear labels literally make your AI smarter about your code. And when a change needs review before it lands, those commits travel together as a pull request — readable labels make the reviewer's job easy.

Rule of thumb: if you can't describe the commit in one short line, it's too big — split it. And write the label for a stranger reading it in six months. "Fix: annual price showed $0" beats "fixed bug" every single time. The stranger is usually you.

What this unlocks for you

  • Pinpoint when a bug appeared — every snapshot is a moment you can inspect and test
  • Undo one change without losing the rest — small commits rewind surgically
  • A readable diary of the project — what changed, when, and why, with zero extra effort
  • A safety net before AI edits — commit, then let Claude go wild; you can always step back
  • A backed-up album — push your commits to GitHub and they outlive your laptop
