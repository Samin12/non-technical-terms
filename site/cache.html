A chef is cooking forty dishes tonight, and thirty of them need garlic. The walk-in fridge is nine meters away. The first trip is unavoidable — but a chef who walks nine meters for every single clove doesn't last the week. The good ones take one trip, prep the garlic, and keep it on the counter.

You just ran that kitchen. Cache off: every request was a full round trip, and the distance tally got embarrassing. Cache on: one slow miss, then instant hit after instant hit.

A cache is a copy of something expensive to fetch, kept close, so the next request is nearly free.

Caches are everywhere — including right now

Your browser cached this page's fonts and styles the first time you visited, which is why the second load feels instant. DNS answers get cached so the internet's phone book isn't re-read for every click. A CDN is caching at planetary scale — whole copies of a site parked near every city. And in the AI world, prompt caching is Claude reading your giant instructions once at full price, then re-serving them for cents. Different layers, one identical move: the garlic is expensive to get, so keep some on the counter.

The hard part: stale garlic

There's an old joke engineers never stop telling: "There are only two hard things in computer science — cache invalidation and naming things." It's funny because it's true, and invalidation is the genuinely hard one.

The moment the real thing changes, your counter copy becomes a lie. Stale. And stale prep is worse than no prep: wrong prices, last month's profile photo, the classic "I fixed the typo but the site still shows it." You pressed the stale button and saw the only honest fix — throw the copy out, walk to the fridge once, cache the fresh version. Easy in a demo. In real systems, knowing when to throw the copy out is where engineers earn their pay.

The rule: expensive, and rarely changing

Cache the logo, not the bank balance. Before caching anything, ask two questions: how expensive is it to fetch, and how often does it change? Expensive and stable — fonts, images, a 50-page system prompt — is a perfect candidate. Cheap or constantly changing — live scores, your account balance — should be fetched fresh, because a stale answer there isn't slow. It's wrong. A cache is a kind of state, after all, and state you forget to update will happily lie to you at full speed.

Next time something "won't update" — your edit isn't showing, the site looks old — suspect the cache before you suspect the code. Hard-refresh the page, clear the cache, or just wait it out. Half of all "it's broken!" reports are a perfectly good fix hiding behind stale garlic.

Slow but right beats fast but wrong — and cached-and-fresh beats them both.
