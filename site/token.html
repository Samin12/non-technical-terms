Token

TLDRThe chunks AI reads and writes in.

Forget letters. Forget words, too.

When you send Claude a sentence, it never sees that sentence the way you do. Before any "thinking" happens, your text gets snapped apart into chunks — like a LEGO wall being pulled back into bricks. A short common word like "the" is one brick. A long word like "understanding" becomes three: under + stand + ing. The tokenizer in the widget below does exactly this to your own typing.

A token is one of those chunks — the unit AI actually reads and writes in. Rule of thumb: one token is about four characters, or three-quarters of a word.

Why chunks instead of words?

Because chunks are reusable. English alone has hundreds of thousands of words — then add names, slang, typos, code, and emoji. No fixed list of words could ever cover all of it. But a fixed menu of a few tens of thousands of well-chosen chunks can spell anything, the same way 26 letters can spell every word you'll ever invent. Bigger pieces than letters, smaller than words. That menu is the model's vocabulary, and everything you type gets assembled from it.

It's also why your AI shrugs off typos and made-up product names. "Biscuitify" isn't in any dictionary, but as bricks — B + iscuit + ify — it's no problem at all.

Everything runs on the token meter

Here's why this lesson is worth ten minutes of your life: tokens are the meter the entire AI world bills, measures, and throttles by.

  • Pricing. AI APIs charge per million tokens — both the tokens you send in and the tokens it writes back. The cost counter in the widget is real math: at $3 per million input tokens, your tweet costs a hundredth of a cent. That 10-page doc, attached to 1,000 requests? Twenty dollars. Quietly.
  • Memory. The context window — how much your AI can hold in mind at once — is a token budget, not a page count.
  • Speed. Models generate one token at a time. A long answer is more tokens, which is more seconds of watching text stream in.

One more wrinkle: output tokens — the words the model writes — are usually priced higher than input tokens. Reading is cheap; writing is premium. Worth knowing before you ask for a 2,000-word answer when a table would have done the job.

Before pasting a giant document "just in case," remember: every token you send costs money, eats context window space, and competes for the model's attention. Paste the page you need, not the manual. And if you genuinely must send the same big document over and over, that's exactly what prompt caching was invented for.

What this unlocks for you

Once you start thinking in tokens, a lot of mysterious AI behavior turns into arithmetic:

  • You can estimate cost before you build: word count × 1.33 ≈ tokens, then multiply by the price sheet
  • You know why an answer cut off mid-sentence — it hit a token limit, not a creativity wall
  • You can read any model's pricing page and actually compare apples to apples
  • You understand why "be concise" is a cost-saving instruction when your app serves thousands of users

Play with the presets above one more time and watch the cost line. A tweet is noise. An email is pocket change. A 10-page doc, sent at scale, is a real line item on your card. Same model, same price sheet — the only variable is bricks.

AI doesn't read your words — it reads your bricks, and it bills you per brick.

✓ Check yourself

1. You paste a 6,000-word report into Claude. Roughly what does the model see?

A token is about three-quarters of a word, so 6,000 words ≈ 8,000 tokens. Every one of them costs money and desk space.

2. Your app sends the same 10-page doc with all 1,000 daily requests. Why is the monthly bill a shock?

You pay for every token going in and coming out. Same-doc-every-time is exactly the bill that prompt caching exists to shrink.

3. Claude handles your made-up brand name "Biscuitify" with zero trouble. How?

The vocabulary is chunks, not words. Anything new is just bricks it already owns, clicked together in a new order.
