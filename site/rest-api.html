Walk into any location of a big chain restaurant, anywhere on earth, and you can order in ten seconds. Menu board in the same place, combos numbered the same way, "make it large" works everywhere. You learned the system once; now every branch is familiar.

That's REST. And the Chaos toggle you just suffered through? That's what the web was like before it — every API a hand-built snowflake, every developer guessing at URLs like /del_cust_FINAL and praying.

REST is the rulebook most APIs follow: name things as nouns, act on them with standard verbs, answer with standard codes.

The three rules that matter

  • Everything is a noun. Orders live at /orders, customers at /customers. One endpoint per thing, named after the thing. No /getAllOrdersNow.php — the URL names what, never how.
  • Actions come from the verbs you already know. There's no /createOrder door, because POST /orders already says it. The whole HTTP Methods lesson plugs straight in here.
  • Receipts are standardized. 200 means here's your data, 201 means created, 404 means no such thing. Even the refusals follow the rulebook — when you tried to DELETE the menu, you got a clean 405 "method not allowed," not a mystery explosion.

Predictability is the feature

Nothing about REST is technically impressive. That's the point. Its entire value is that it's boring in the same way everywhere. In the widget, you composed nine different requests without reading a single page of documentation — you knew GET /customers would list customers before you clicked, the same way you know where the fries are on the menu board.

Compare that to your Chaos run: five cryptic URLs, one of which silently deleted a customer while you were just trying to read the list. Pre-REST, that wasn't a joke — every integration started with archaeology. REST won because it replaced guessing with a grammar. It's a convention, not a law — you'll meet APIs that bend it — but the default assumption is right so often that it's the first thing to try.

Why your AI can drive APIs it has never seen

Here's where this stops being trivia. Claude has absorbed thousands of REST APIs, and because they all share the grammar, it can operate one it's never met. Hand it the docs for some brand-new service and it already knows the shape: nouns for resources, verbs for actions, 201 when something's created. That's a huge part of why AI agents can wire into your tools in minutes — and why an SDK can wrap any of these APIs so neatly. Predictable rules are automatable rules.

Meeting a new API? Find the list of resources in the docs first — the nouns. Once you know a service thinks in /contacts, /deals, and /notes, you can usually predict 80% of its endpoints before reading another page. The nouns are the map.

What this unlocks for you

  • Debug from status codes: 404 = wrong noun, 405 = wrong verb at the right noun

2. Why can your AI operate an API it has never encountered before?

It's not memorization — it's grammar. Once you (or Claude) know nouns + verbs + standard codes, a brand-new API is just new vocabulary in a familiar language.

3. A request comes back 201. Without reading anything else, what almost certainly just happened?

Standard codes are part of the rulebook: 201 Created follows a successful "make a new one" request. Deletions usually answer 204, and crashes live in the 500s.
