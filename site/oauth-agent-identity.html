OAuth & Agent Identity

TLDRA valet key for your accounts. Not the master key.

When you valet-park a nice car, you don't hand over your whole keyring. You hand over the valet key: it starts the engine and opens the driver's door, and that's it. No trunk, no glovebox, definitely not your house. The valet gets exactly enough key to do the job.

Now look at what people do with software: an app asks for access, and they type in their actual password. That's not the valet key. That's the entire keyring, copied, handed to a stranger.

OAuth is how you hand an app — or your AI agent — a limited, revocable key to your account instead of your password.

You'll watch the difference in the widget below. Master key: inbox gone in two seconds, and nothing to revoke afterward. Valet key: a clean "403 — scope not granted," and 1,283 emails still standing.

Scopes: the teeth on the key

Each permission on an OAuth key is called a scope, and each scope is one door: read the inbox, send as you, delete. That consent screen you've clicked a hundred times — "This app wants to: view your email, send email on your behalf" — is the locksmith's order form. You're choosing which teeth get cut into the key.

The 403 in the widget wasn't the agent being polite. The key physically lacked the delete teeth, so the door wouldn't open — same fence logic as the API menu: what isn't offered can't be done, no matter how the requester asks. That's the deepest idea in modern security: don't trust the borrower's behavior, shape the key. Good behavior is a hope; a missing scope is a guarantee. And like every credential, these tokens live in your env file, never in the code itself.

Why agents make this urgent

A chatbot just reads your words. An agent acts as you — it sends mail with your name on it, moves your files, spends your money. That's why "agent identity" is becoming its own discipline, and it boils down to one rule: every agent gets its own valet key.

  • Audit trail: the log reads "inbox-agent sent this at 3:14 PM" — not just "you did something"
  • Blast radius: one agent misbehaves, you revoke one key — the others keep working, like the clean kill you saw on the Revoke button
  • Least privilege: the research agent gets read-only; only the outreach agent can send

One agent, one key, one leash. As your AI workforce grows, this is the difference between "manageable" and "pray nothing goes wrong."

Red flag: any tool that asks you to type your actual password into its own settings page. Real OAuth bounces you to the service's official consent screen — you log in at google.com, never inside the app — and shows you the scope list. No consent screen, no deal.

Your 60-second security habit

  • Read the scope list before clicking Allow — does a meme generator really need "delete"?
  • Grant the minimum: read-only until the tool earns more
  • Know your revoke page (for Google: Security → third-party access) and prune it quarterly
  • One key per agent — never let two agents share a credential

None of this slows you down. Granting a scoped key takes the same ten seconds as pasting a password — it just doesn't cost you the whole account when something goes sideways.

Hand out valet keys all day. The master key never leaves your pocket.

✓ Check yourself

1. A new AI email tool asks you to enter your Gmail password on its own settings page. What's the OAuth-savvy move?

A legit integration never touches your password. You log in at the service itself, approve specific scopes, and the app receives only a limited token.

2. In the widget, the haywire agent held a valet key without the delete scope — and the inbox survived. Why?

Safety came from the key, not the agent's judgment. A scope you never grant is a door that cannot open — even on the agent's worst day.

3. You stop trusting one of your three agents. With proper per-agent OAuth identity, what do you do?

That's the whole point of one-key-per-agent: surgical revocation. You saw it — revoke, and the very next request dies cleanly with a 401.
