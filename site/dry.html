DRY

TLDRWrite it once. Reuse it everywhere.

Imagine mailing fifty wedding invitations and hand-writing your return address on every envelope. Then you move. Fifty envelopes to fix — and you will miss one. The fix is a rubber stamp: change the stamp once, and every future envelope is automatically right.

You just lived the painful half of that in Round 1. The support email was copy-pasted into five corners of the site, and the receipt template — the one scrolled out of view — is exactly the kind of copy that gets missed in real projects. Round 2 was the stamp: one constant, one click, five fixes, zero misses.

DRY — "Don't Repeat Yourself" — means every fact in your project lives in exactly one place, and everything else points to it.

Duplication is how bugs breed

Here is the thing about that missed receipt: it wasn't a typo, and nobody was careless. The information was simply stored five times, and five copies means five separate chances to forget one. Every duplicate is a little time bomb with no countdown display.

Worse, the bug is invisible. The site looked fixed — header, footer, contact page all showed the new address. The broken copy only surfaced when a customer hit it, which is the most expensive possible way to find out. Multiply this by every price, label, rule, and address in a real product, and you understand why developers treat copy-paste like a smell.

The fixed version has a name worth knowing: a single source of truth. The email lives in one constant. The price lives in one place. Change the truth, and everything that references it updates for free. You've already met this idea twice in this course: your env file is a single source of truth for secrets, and CLAUDE.md is one for your AI's house rules.

How to direct your AI with it

DRY is a phrase you say, not a technique you study. When you're reviewing something Claude built — or something you vibe-coded last month and barely remember — ask:

  • "Is this email / price / name defined in more than one place? Make it DRY."
  • "If this value changes next year, how many edits is that?" The right answer is one.
  • "Extract the duplicated logic into one function."

Claude is genuinely great at this job. Spotting copy-paste across a whole project is tedious for humans and trivial for an AI that can read every file at once. And the cleanup itself is a classic refactoring move — improving the structure without changing the behavior.

The trap: over-DRY

Now the counterweight, because every principle has one. DRY applies to things that are genuinely the same fact. It does not mean "merge everything that looks similar."

Say your receipt email and your marketing email share most of their formatting. Merge them into one template and you've tied two boats together: the moment marketing wants something receipts can't have, you're untangling the knot at the worst possible time. They looked alike by coincidence — but they change for different reasons. Keep them separate.

Rule of thumb to tell Claude: "Only merge these if they would always change together." A little honest duplication beats the wrong abstraction. The third-time rule helps too: copy something once, fine. Twice, fine. The third time, extract it into one place.

What this unlocks for you

  • Change a price, a name, or an address once — and trust it changed everywhere
  • Review AI output with one sharp question: "single source of truth?"
  • Kill the "I thought we fixed that" class of bug entirely
  • Pair it with KISS: readable code with no clones is code you can actually own

Fix it once and it's fixed. Fix it in five places and you've planted four future bugs.
