Picture an office building at 2 AM. Every office is dark, every desk empty. But the security guard is doing rounds, the boiler is keeping the pipes warm, and the night cleaner is on floor six. The building never actually sleeps — only the visible part of it does.

Your computer is that building. The apps you see — windows, buttons, dock icons — are the daytime offices. Underneath, dozens of programs with no windows at all are doing rounds: syncing the clock, watching the wifi, fetching mail, backing things up.

A daemon is a process with no window and no UI that runs in the background — usually starting when the machine starts, and never exiting. It's pronounced "DEE-mun," and no, it's not evil. It's old programmer humor for a helpful spirit that works unseen.

You already depend on a dozen of them

You never "open" a daemon. There's nothing to click. The operating system launches them at boot and they just… stay. Notification arriving? A daemon was listening. Dropbox icon says "synced"? A daemon watched your folders. Clock always right? A daemon — literally one of the rows in the panel above — has been quietly correcting drift the whole time you've been reading. Open Activity Monitor on a Mac and count: hundreds of processes, and the overwhelming majority have no window you could point to. That's the basement staff.

That's why the log-out button in the widget is the whole lesson. You left. Every window closed. And the uptimes kept counting, the log kept streaming. Daemons don't belong to your windows or your mood — they belong to the machine.

Crashes, supervisors, and uptime

You probably caught backup-d dying in the panel. Status: crashed. Two seconds later: running again, uptime reset to zero. Nobody clicked anything.

That's the other half of daemon life: a supervisor — itself a daemon — watches every service and restarts whatever falls over. macOS calls its supervisor launchd; Linux calls it systemd. This is how machines run for months unattended: not because nothing ever crashes, but because something is always standing by to restart what does. Daemon people brag about uptime the way runners brag about streaks — "up 247 days" is a badge of honor.

Why this matters when you ship

Here's the connection to your own projects: when you deploy a web app, your code becomes a daemon. It's a process on a server with no screen attached — that's headless, the next lesson — answering requests at 3 AM, auto-restarted when it crashes. "Always on" isn't magic. It's a daemon, plus a supervisor, plus a machine that never closes.

Once you know the word, you can ask for the right thing:

  • "Run it as a service" means: run it as a daemon — background, supervised, restarted on crash
  • A Telegram bot or webhook listener that must be always-on? That's a daemon job, not a leave-the-laptop-open job
  • Activity Monitor full of names you don't recognize? Mostly daemons. Mostly fine.
  • Your scheduled AI agents lean on the oldest daemon of all — cron, the cron job lesson is two stops away
Warning: daemons fail silently. No window means no error popup — a dead background service just stops, and you find out days later when the backups are missing. The builder's rule: every daemon must write logs, and you must know where they are. Logs are a daemon's only voice.

Your apps work shifts. Daemons work forever.

✓ Check yourself

1. You close every app and log out of your Mac. Which of these keeps working?

Daemons don't belong to your windows or your login session. No UI, nothing to close — they keep ticking, exactly like the panel did after you hit log out.

2. A backup service crashes on a healthy server at 3 AM. What usually happens next?

Supervisors (launchd on macOS, systemd on Linux) exist precisely for this: watch every service, restart what falls over. That's the auto-restart that hit backup-d in the widget.

3. You built a Telegram bot that must respond 24/7, but your laptop sleeps at night. What do you actually need?

A sleeping laptop pauses its processes. Always-on work belongs on an always-on machine, running as a supervised background service — a daemon.
