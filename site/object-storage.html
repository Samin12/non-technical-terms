You don't buy a bigger garage every time you buy a kayak. You rent a storage unit — the magic kind that grows whenever you show up with more stuff, where every box gets its own labeled spot so you can find it again in two years.

That's the choice you just made in the widget. The server's disk was the garage: three files in, it was full — and because your app lives in that same garage, the whole site went down with it. The bucket didn't flinch. It swallowed a 4 GB backup and handed you back an address.

Object storage is a giant, effectively bottomless bucket on the internet: you drop a file in, and it gets kept — with its own URL — for as long as you want.

Objects and addresses, not folders

Every file in the bucket is called an object: the file, its name, and its address. No drive letters, no nested folder maze, no "which machine did I save that on?" The video simply lives at bucket.club/video.mp4, and anything that knows the address can fetch it — your website, your app, your AI.

Compare that to the disk inside a server. It's a fixed size, and your app, your logs, and your operating system are all elbowing for room on it. When uploads fill it, nothing else fits — and the site dies, exactly like the meter you just maxed out. Worse: on many modern hosts, that disk gets wiped clean every time you deploy. Files you "saved" there last week quietly vanish with the next ship.

What belongs in the bucket

Short answer: everything heavy that isn't code.

  • User uploads — profile photos, receipts, attachments
  • Media — podcast audio, video files, every image on your site
  • Backups — database snapshots, exports, just-in-case copies
  • AI output — when Claude generates fifty carousel images for you, they belong in a bucket, not on your laptop

The pattern is always the same: your app stays small. Code lives on the server, structured records live in the database — and the database stores only the file's URL, a short string, while the actual 900 MB video sits in the bucket. Skinny app, heavy bucket.

Pro tip: never store user uploads on the same disk your app runs on. It's one of the most common ways small projects die — the disk fills silently for months, then one Tuesday the site is down and the server can't even write the error log, because that needs disk too. Buckets cost a few cents a month. Downtime costs you trust.

Cheap, and built to be fetched

You watched the cost ticker: at roughly $0.02 per GB per month, all four files — nearly 5 GB — totaled about a dime. That's the other superpower of object storage: it's absurdly cheap, so you never have to think about deleting things to make room.

And buckets are built to serve, not just store. Pair one with a CDN, which keeps copies of your most-fetched objects close to every visitor, and that video starts instantly in Tokyo and São Paulo alike — while your server does nothing at all. That's the standard setup behind almost every site you use: small app, big bucket, fast copies everywhere.

Keep your app skinny. Let the bucket carry everything heavy.

✓ Check yourself

1. Your app lets customers upload receipts, and you're saving them to the server's own disk. What happens as the business grows?

A server disk is finite and shared with the app itself — you watched it die at three files. Uploads belong in a bucket that grows forever.

2. A 900 MB launch video sits in your bucket at bucket.club/launch.mp4. How does your website show it to visitors?

Every object has its own address. Your page embeds that URL, the bucket (plus a CDN) serves the bytes, and your server never touches the video.

3. Claude just generated 300 product images for your store. Where should they live?

Heavy files go in the bucket; the database stores only the short address strings. Your app stays small, and 300 images cost you about a penny a month.
