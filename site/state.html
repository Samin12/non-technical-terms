A whiteboard and a notebook. The whiteboard is fast — scribble anything, it's right there, instantly. It's also one sleeve-brush away from oblivion. The notebook is slower to write in, but it survives the night, the week, the building's power going out.

Every piece of software you use is juggling those two surfaces, all the time. You just felt the difference in the crash test: same draft, same crash, completely different morning after.

State is everything your software remembers right now — your half-typed draft, your cart, your login, the count on a counter.

Memory is the whiteboard

A running program — a process — keeps its working state in memory (RAM). That's the whiteboard: ridiculously fast, perfect for the thousand little things an app tracks while you use it. And it is wiped the instant the process dies. Not "sometimes." Always — that's what RAM is.

In memory-only mode you typed a draft, hit crash, and got back a blank box. The draft wasn't deleted. It was never durable to begin with. It lived on a whiteboard, and the process took the whiteboard down with it.

Saving is writing it in the notebook

Flip to saved mode and every keystroke was also written through to a database on disk. Crash, reboot — and the app reads your draft back letter for letter. A hair slower on every write, infinitely better on the one day it matters. This is why real apps autosave, why your cart survives a refresh, why "draft restored" feels like magic but is really just state that lived in the notebook instead of on the whiteboard.

The click counter obeyed the same law, because state isn't just text — it's every value the app would need to pick up exactly where you left off. The status line in the widget told you, at every moment, where your state lived: RAM or disk. Engineers track precisely that, for every piece of data they handle.

Why "turn it off and on again" works

The oldest joke in IT is actually state management. Sometimes a program's in-memory state goes bad — it's wedged halfway through something, holding a value that no longer makes sense. A restart wipes the whiteboard and reloads clean, trusted state from disk. That's the entire trick.

It's also why serious systems run "stateless" servers: the server keeps nothing precious in its own memory. Your login lives in a session store, your data in the database. Any server can die mid-request and a clone steps in, because the notebook was never inside the server to begin with.

Assume the crash. Before you trust any app — including the ones you vibe-code yourself — ask the whiteboard question: "if this process died right now, what survives?" Anything you'd cry about losing belongs on disk, not in RAM.

✓ Check yourself

1. You typed half an email, the app crashed, and after reload your draft came back perfectly. What must be true?

RAM dies with the process — no restart is fast enough and no data is small enough to escape that. Only state written to disk survives a crash.

2. Support says "try turning it off and on again" — and it actually works. What did the restart do?

A restart is a whiteboard wipe. Whatever wedged, half-finished state the program was holding in RAM is gone, and it boots fresh from what's saved.

3. An engineer says "our servers are stateless." What do they mean?

Stateless doesn't mean no state — it means the state lives elsewhere. The notebook sits outside the server, so servers become replaceable.
