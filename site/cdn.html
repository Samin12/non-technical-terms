Visit every city with the CDN off, then flip it on and visit again. That gap in the table is why the internet feels fast.

Imagine your pizza place makes the best pie on Earth — but the only kitchen is in Virginia. An order comes in from Tokyo. Even with the fastest courier alive, that pizza is crossing an ocean. It arrives cold. It always arrives cold, no matter how good the kitchen is, because the problem was never the kitchen. It's the distance.

Pizza chains figured out the fix long before the internet did: open kitchens everywhere, same recipe. Whoever orders gets pizza from the kitchen nearest them.

A CDN — content delivery network — keeps copies of your site in dozens of cities, so every visitor downloads from somewhere close.

Distance is the speed limit

Data travels at nearly the speed of light, and it is still too slow. You watched it on the map: Tokyo to the Virginia origin server and back is roughly 280 milliseconds — and a single page load makes dozens of those round trips for images, fonts, and scripts. Stack them up and "across the ocean" becomes a site that feels heavy and slow.

Flip the CDN on and the same visit hits a copy a few hundred miles away instead: ~25 milliseconds. Nothing about your site changed. The files just stopped commuting.

How the copies actually work

CDNs shine for static files — images, video, CSS, fonts, this very lesson page. Files that are identical for every visitor are perfect to photocopy.

The first visitor in each city does a little extra work. Tokyo's node doesn't have your homepage yet, so it fetches it from the origin once, hands it over, and keeps the copy. That's called warming the cache — and every Tokyo visitor after that gets the fast version. One slow trip buys a whole city's speed. You saw it as the "origin trips saved" counter climbing.

The flip side: copies can briefly run stale. Update your logo, and a visitor in London might see the old one for a few minutes until London's copy refreshes. If a friend ever swears your update "isn't live" while you're staring right at it — it's live. Their copy is just a beat behind.

You're probably already using one

The platforms you deploy to — the Vercels and Netlifys of the world — quietly put a CDN in front of every site they host. That's a big part of why everything you ship feels instantly fast worldwide, and why skipping it (serving big images from one lonely server) is something your visitors in Sydney feel immediately. DNS even plays along: the name resolves to whichever copy sits nearest the visitor.

Pro tip: if your site is snappy at home but "slow" in another country, don't rewrite your code. Your files are just far away. Putting a CDN in front is usually a checkbox on your host, not a project — check that box before you refactor anything.

What this unlocks for you

  • Diagnose "it's slow in Australia" in one sentence
  • Ship the image-heavy site Claude built you without melting one poor server
  • Understand what hosts mean when they brag about their "global network"
  • Stay calm when a just-deployed change looks stale to someone else

And when you want to run code in all those cities — not just store files there — that's a later lesson: the edge.

One kitchen can't feed the planet. Copies can. That's the whole CDN.

✓ Check yourself

1. Your site loads instantly for you in New York, but visitors in Sydney call it "painfully slow." What's the likely fix?

The code is fine — the files are 15,000 km away. Copies close to visitors remove the distance, which was the actual problem.

2. The first visitor from Tokyo waits 300 ms, but every Tokyo visitor after that waits 25 ms. What happened?

That first slow request "warms" the local cache. One origin trip buys fast loads for the entire city afterward.

3. You deploy a new logo, but a friend in London still sees the old one for a few minutes. Why?

A CDN serves stored copies, and copies refresh on a short delay after a deploy. Stale-for-a-moment is normal, not broken.
