Worker

TLDRA small program that runs one job on demand.

Friday night. A restaurant kitchen. Orders are flying in, and the head chef is running the line — reading tickets, plating dishes, keeping the whole room moving.

So who's peeling the potatoes? Who's dicing the forty pounds of onions? Not the head chef. That's what prep cooks are for. Each one takes a ticket, does one thing well, and grabs the next ticket. The chef never stops running the line.

That's a worker: a small program that sits off to the side, picks up one job at a time, runs it, and asks for the next.

Hand off the ticket, keep moving

Your app — the part people actually click on — is the head chef. Its whole job is staying responsive. When you upload 400 photos, it does not stop to resize them while you stare at a spinner. It writes 400 tickets, drops them on a queue, instantly says "got it," and goes back to answering clicks. Off to the side, workers grab tickets and chew through them.

You just played this exact scene in the dispatcher above: the pending lane is the queue, each slot in the pool is a worker, and every ✓ is one finished job. The app never broke a sweat — because the app never did the heavy lifting. That handoff — answer now, work later — is the same instinct you'll meet again in async.

Scaling means more cooks, not a faster chef

Here's the beautiful part: throughput becomes a knob. When the line of pending tasks gets long, you don't rewrite your app or shop for a magic computer. You add workers. You watched it happen — ten tasks with one worker is a slow, sad line; the same ten tasks with five workers evaporate.

Engineers call this scaling horizontally, and modern platforms do it automatically: serverless systems spin up extra workers when the line grows and quietly dismiss them when things calm down. Demand spikes stop being emergencies. They're just longer lines that more cooks eat through.

This is what "background processing" means

Every time an app tells you "we're processing your file — we'll email you when it's ready," you now know exactly what's happening: a worker has your ticket. AI tools transcribing an hour of audio, video exports, "generating your report," Claude running a long task while you keep chatting — workers, all the way down. There's nothing magical back there. Just small programs with one skill each, doing it ten thousand times without complaining.

Pro-tip: keep workers boring. One worker, one kind of job. And design for death — a worker should be able to crash mid-task and have another worker simply retry the ticket. Nothing lost, nobody paged. That retry safety net is the heart of the Job lesson, coming right up.

What this unlocks for you

  • The upload that "processes in the background" while you keep browsing — workers
  • The "your export is ready" email that lands four minutes later — a worker finished your ticket
  • AI transcription that runs without freezing the page — a worker, not the chatbot itself
  • Apps that stay snappy under load — the heavy stuff was never in your way to begin with

Your app's job is to answer the door. The workers' job is everything else.

✓ Check yourself

1. You upload 300 photos and the app instantly says "Processing — we'll notify you," and lets you keep browsing. What's happening backstage?

An instant answer plus results-later is the worker signature: the main app handed off tickets and went straight back to answering clicks.

2. It's launch day. The pending lane grows all afternoon, and your single worker is healthy but maxed out. The classic fix?

Exactly what you saw in the dispatcher: same ten tasks, five workers instead of one. Resetting the line loses work, and the main app must stay free to answer the door.

3. Why shouldn't the main app — the one answering every click — also transcode uploaded videos itself?

It physically could — it just shouldn't. A head chef peeling potatoes means the whole line stops. Heavy work goes to workers so the app stays responsive.
