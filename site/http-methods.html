You already use four verbs at a library without thinking: look at a book, donate a new one, swap in a fresh edition, pull a damaged copy off the shelf. Four moves. That's the entire repertoire.

The web runs on the same four moves. Every time any app talks to any other app — your AI included — the request leads with a verb that declares its intent.

HTTP methods are the verbs of the web: GET reads, POST creates, PUT updates, DELETE removes.

Same door, different verb

In the Endpoint lesson you learned the nouns — the doors, like /orders. Methods are what you do at the door. You just felt this in the playground: GET /orders and POST /orders hit the exact same address, and one left the board untouched while the other grew it. Verb plus noun makes a complete sentence: POST /orders literally reads "create an order."

  • GET — read it. Loading your profile, fetching a list. Never changes anything. You can GET all day with zero consequences.
  • POST — create something new. A signup, a payment, a new order. Every POST makes another one — you watched #4, #5, #6 stack up.
  • PUT — update what's there. (Its cousin PATCH edits just one field.) Order #2 went large; run it again and it's still just… large. Same call, same result.
  • DELETE — remove it. Order #3 faded out. Try deleting it twice and the second attempt 404s — it's already gone.

The numbers that came back are the receipts: 200 means "done, here you go," 201 means "created your new thing," 204 means "deleted, nothing left to show you." You'll meet the full family in the REST API lesson — for now, just notice the answers were as predictable as the verbs.

Why this matters when you build with AI

When Claude wires up an automation through an API, every step in its log is one of these sentences: GET /subscribers, POST /invoices, DELETE /webhooks/3. Once you read verbs, those logs stop being noise and start being a story — it read the list, it created an invoice, it removed a hook. You can audit your own agent at a glance.

It also sharpens how you ask. "Pull the rows but don't modify anything" is you saying GET only. "Add this as a new contact" is a POST. Speaking in verbs gets you exactly the automation you meant, instead of roughly the one you described.

The verb tells you what's safe to retry. Retrying a GET is free — reading twice changes nothing. Retrying a POST creates a second thing: two orders, two invoices, two charges on a customer's card. When an automation "mysteriously made duplicates," a POST got fired twice. It's also why checkout pages beg you not to click the buy button again.

What this unlocks for you

  • Read any request log like English — verb + door = what just happened
  • Brief your AI precisely: "GET the data, never POST" is a one-line safety rail
  • Diagnose duplicates instantly (something retried a POST)
  • Know which buttons are safe to mash (reads) and which are not (creates)

Nouns are the doors. Verbs are what you do at them. Four verbs run the entire conversation between every app on the internet.

✓ Check yourself

1. Your AI needs to pull your last 10 Shopify orders to summarize them — changing nothing. Which request should it send?

Summarizing is pure reading, and reading is GET. POST would create a new order, and DELETE would be a very bad morning.

2. Your invoicing automation glitched and every customer got two identical invoices. Which verb was almost certainly involved?

POST is the "make another one" verb. A retried GET reads twice and changes nothing; a retried POST manufactures a second invoice.

3. You want to fix just the shipping address on order #7 — everything else stays. Best fit?

Updating an existing thing is PUT/PATCH territory — aimed at the one specific order, /orders/7. GET can't change data, and DELETE is a sledgehammer.
