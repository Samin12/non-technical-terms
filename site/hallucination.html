All three answers arrive in the same confident voice. That's the entire problem.

Everyone has that friend. Ask them anything — anything — and the answer comes back instantly, fluently, with total conviction. Capital of Mongolia? Tax rules for freelancers? How to fix your boiler? Instant answer. The one sentence you will never hear from them: "I don't know."

Great fun at dinner. Genuinely dangerous if you're using their answers to file your taxes. Your AI is that friend.

A hallucination is when AI states something false with complete confidence — not lying, just generating plausible-sounding text without checking it against reality.

Why it happens

Under the hood, a language model does one thing: predict the next token — the most plausible next chunk of text, given everything so far. Plausible. Not verified. There's no fact-checking pass, no database lookup, no little librarian confirming sources before the words come out.

For famous, well-documented facts — the Eiffel Tower, Python's creator — "most plausible" and "true" are the same thing, which is why two of the three answers in the game were solid. But ask about something obscure, and the model still produces text shaped exactly like a confident answer, because that's the only shape it knows. That fake Notion endpoint, /v1/exportAll, wasn't random noise. It's precisely what an export endpoint should look like. Hallucinations aren't badly formed — they're too well formed.

The high-risk zones

You don't need to verify everything. You need to know where to aim your skepticism:

  • Citations and quotes — papers, court cases, and book quotes that simply don't exist
  • API names, endpoints, and functions — plausible code referencing things that were never written
  • Numbers and statistics — suspiciously precise figures with no source attached
  • Legal, medical, and financial specifics — anywhere a wrong answer costs real money or health

And one multiplier: answers get shakier when the model is working from a stuffed, overflowing context window — or from no source material at all.

How builders shrink it

  • Hand it the source. Paste the actual policy, the real API docs, the contract itself. When the truth is sitting in the conversation, the AI quotes instead of inventing.
  • Invite "I don't know." Literally add: "If you're not sure, say so." It works far better than it has any right to.
  • Let it check itself. An agent can run the code, call the real endpoint, and watch it fail — actions ground it in reality in a way pure chat never can.
  • Human eyes before it ships. Anything from the high-risk list gets verified before it touches a customer, a contract, or production.
The danger is asymmetric: hallucinations hurt most exactly where you can't judge the answer yourself — if you could spot the error, you wouldn't have needed to ask. So flip your instinct. The more specific and confident an answer looks in territory you don't know, the more it has earned a fact-check.

What this unlocks for you

  • You stop treating AI output as either gospel or garbage — you triage it
  • You know the four zones that always get a second look
  • Your prompts improve overnight: sources in, "say if you're unsure" on
  • You can explain to your team why the AI "lied" — it didn't; it autocompleted

That's the real takeaway from the game you just played: you couldn't tell by tone, and you never will. The fake endpoint sounded exactly as sure of itself as the Eiffel Tower.

Confidence is the model's writing style, not its evidence. Enjoy the style — verify the substance.

✓ Check yourself

1. Claude hands you a perfectly formatted court case for your dispute letter: "Henderson v. Atlantic Freight (2019)". Before you use it?

Fake citations are formatted exactly like real ones, and a model can confidently "confirm" its own invention. Citations are a verify-always zone.

2. Why does the AI invent an API endpoint instead of just saying "I don't know"?

No lookup happens — just next-chunk prediction. Unless you invite uncertainty, "confident answer" is the most statistically likely shape for the text to take.

3. You want trustworthy answers about your refund policy. The single best move?

Grounding beats vibes. With the source sitting on the desk, the AI quotes your policy instead of generating a plausible-sounding one.
