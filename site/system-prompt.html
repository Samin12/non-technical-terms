System Prompt

TLDRThe standing instructions your AI always follows.

Before a single guest walks in, every employee at a good hotel has read the handbook. How to greet. What tone to use. What never to say. Guests never see the handbook — they just experience the staff behaving in a very particular way.

Your AI has a handbook too. You just played with it: same model, same complaint about a missing order — and three completely different employees showed up, depending on which standing orders were active.

The system prompt is the set of standing instructions an AI receives before the conversation starts — and follows for all of it.

Same model, same question — the only thing that changed was the standing orders. That’s the entire trick behind most “AI personalities.”

Invisible, and always on

Two things make system prompts special. First: the end user never sees them. When you chat with a support bot, you see your messages and its answers — not the page of instructions sitting behind the curtain telling it to stay cheerful, never discuss competitors, and always mention the warranty.

Second: it never leaves. The system prompt sits in the context window — the AI’s desk — for the entire conversation. Your chat messages can fall off as the desk fills up, but the standing orders stay pinned, shaping the first reply and the five-hundredth exactly the same way.

That checkbox you ticked — “always end by offering next steps” — wasn’t a new feature or a smarter model. It was one sentence added to the standing orders, and every persona’s behavior changed instantly. That’s the leverage.

The first place to look when AI acts “off”

When an AI product behaves strangely — refuses harmless requests, answers in a weird tone, keeps pushing one product — non-builders blame “the AI.” Builders check the system prompt first. Nine times out of ten the odd behavior was written down somewhere: an instruction that’s too broad, two rules quietly contradicting each other, a persona fighting the task.

This is also why the same model feels different across apps. Claude inside a coding tool and Claude inside a writing app share a brain — they’re reading different handbooks. The same goes for any agent you build: its judgment, caution, and voice all start from the standing orders you give it. Writing one is half the product

Here’s the open secret about a lot of AI products: there is no secret. Plenty of “AI apps” are a system prompt and a payment form. The model is the same one anyone can use — the differentiation is a tightly written handbook nobody else can see: tone, rules, output format, edge cases, what to do when the user is angry. If you can write a great employee handbook, you can build a great AI product.

Write system prompts for an employee who takes everything literally. “Be concise” is mush. “Answer in 3 bullets or fewer unless asked for detail” is a rule. If your AI’s behavior feels vague, read your own orders — you probably wrote vague ones.

What this unlocks for you

  • Build a support bot with your refund policy and tone baked in — no training required
  • Make every assistant on your team answer in the company voice
  • Change “the personality” in one place instead of editing a thousand conversations
  • Debug weird AI behavior by reading the orders instead of guessing at the model

The model is the actor. The system prompt is the role — and you’re the casting director.
