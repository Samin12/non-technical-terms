Picture a dinner party.

At your table, you tell the story of how your dog ate an entire birthday cake. Big laughs. For the rest of the night, that table keeps riffing on it — someone toasts the dog, someone asks for a photo. Then you switch tables. At the new one, you mention "the cake incident" and get blank stares. They're not being rude. They just weren't there.

A session is one continuous conversation with your AI — one table. Everything it "remembers," it remembers only inside that conversation.

That's the whole demo above. Session #1 knew Biscuit. Session #2 had never heard of him. Same Claude, same you, three seconds apart — but the table changed, so the memory was gone.

Where the "memory" actually lives

Here's the part most people get wrong: the model doesn't learn anything from talking to you. Its brain is frozen — chatting doesn't update it. What feels like memory is just the running transcript of your conversation, which the AI re-reads every single time it replies. That transcript sits in the context window, the AI's working desk.

Start a new session and the transcript starts blank. Biscuit wasn't "deleted" — he was never stored anywhere except the conversation itself.

What counts as "new"? Closing the chat and opening another. Hitting /clear in Claude Code. Opening a second conversation in another tab — that's a different table too, even while the first one is still going. The boundary is the conversation, not the day, the app, or your account.

Fresh sessions are a feature, not a bug

It's tempting to keep one giant chat running forever so the AI "knows everything." Builders do the opposite. Long sessions fill up with clutter — old tangents, dead ends, three abandoned versions of the plan — and answers get noticeably worse, until compaction has to squeeze it all down. A fresh session is a clean desk: the AI's full attention on the one thing you're asking right now.

The working rhythm: one task, one session. New task? New table.

So how do builders give AI a memory?

They don't make the model remember. They make the briefing automatic. Durable facts — your stack, your style, your dog's name if it matters — go into files the AI reads at the start of every session. In Claude's world that's CLAUDE.md and memory files: a one-page "previously on…" card handed to every new table before you say a word.

If you catch yourself re-explaining the same three things in every new chat — who you are, what the project is, how you like your answers — stop typing and write them into a CLAUDE.md once. Every future session starts already briefed. Five minutes of setup buys you months of skipped intros.

What this unlocks for you

  • The AI "forgetting" stops feeling random — you know exactly where memory starts and ends
  • You stop nursing one giant immortal chat — and start clean, sharp, per-task sessions
  • Repeat context moves into files instead of being re-typed every morning
  • You learn the escape hatch: when a long session gets confused, starting fresh usually beats arguing with it

Run the widget one more time and watch the counter: one thing known, then zero. That little number is the most honest description of AI memory you'll ever see.

Your AI isn't forgetful — it just sat down at a new table. Your job is to hand that table its briefing.
