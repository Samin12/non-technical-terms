Embedding

TLDRMeaning, turned into numbers.

Quick question: how far is "dog" from "puppy"?

Not in the dictionary — in meaning. Your gut answers instantly: basically next door. And "dog" to "bus"? Different city. You've been carrying a map of meaning in your head your whole life. The widget below is that same map, drawn by a machine.

An embedding is a long list of numbers that pins a piece of text to a spot on a giant map of meaning. Similar meaning, neighboring spots. That's the whole idea — and it quietly powers half of modern AI.

How words get GPS coordinates

An embedding model reads absurd amounts of text and notices which words show up in the same situations. "Puppy" keeps appearing around food bowls, vets, and leashes — and so does "kitten" — so they get parked in the same neighborhood. Nobody labels anything. Location is learned from the company a word keeps.

That's why "wolf" landed next to the pets without anyone telling the model it's an animal: it gets used like the animal words. And "sad" snuggled up to "gloomy" while keeping its distance from "joyful" — you saw the exact numbers when you clicked.

Click around the map and the pattern holds everywhere: "king" grabs "queen" and "prince", "car" grabs "truck". The model never took a vocabulary test. It watched how humans use words, millions of times, and the neighborhoods emerged on their own.

It's not just single words, either. A sentence, a support ticket, a whole page — anything made of tokens can be squashed into one point on the map. "I want my money back" gets coordinates too.

The real map has thousands of dimensions

The widget is two-dimensional because your eyes give up at three. Real embeddings are lists of a thousand-plus numbers — one point in a thousand-dimensional space. Each number is one dial: a little "is it alive?", a pinch of "is it formal?", a dash of "is it positive?" No single dial means much on its own. Together, they triangulate a meaning.

You can't picture that space. Nobody can. You also don't need to: a computer measures distance in 1,536 dimensions as casually as in two, and it's the same math as the readout you clicked. Close points, close meanings.

This is the unlock: similar becomes a number you can compute. "Refund please" and "I want my money back" share almost no words — their points sit practically on top of each other. Your AI doesn't match spelling. It measures distance.

Different models draw different maps. Coordinates from one embedding model mean nothing on another model's map — compare them and you'll get confident nonsense. Pick one model and use it for everything you store, or re-embed everything when you switch.

What this unlocks for you

  • Search that survives synonyms, typos, and "I don't know what it's called"
  • Clustering thousands of customer messages into themes — automatically
  • "More like this" recommendations for products, videos, articles
  • Spotting the duplicate question that's been asked fifty different ways
  • The storage half: pour these points into a vector database and you can search millions of them in milliseconds — that's the next lesson

So when Claude pulls up the right document even though you butchered every keyword, this is what happened: your sentence became numbers, the documents already were numbers, and something measured the gap between them.

Once meaning has coordinates, software can do geometry on ideas.

✓ Check yourself

1. You embed "I want my money back" and "requesting a refund". What do their two points look like on the meaning map?

Embeddings encode meaning, not spelling. Near-synonyms land near each other — identical is rare, nearby is the whole point.

2. The widget's map was flat (2-D), but real embedding models output 1,000+ numbers per text. Why so many?

Two dimensions can't hold "pet vs. wild vs. royal vs. gloomy" all at once. Thousands of dials can. We flatten to 2-D only so you can look at it.

3. You embedded your docs with model A. Later you embed search queries with model B and compare them. Results are garbage. Why?

Each embedding model has its own coordinate system. Mixing them is comparing GPS positions from two different planets. Stick to one model, or re-embed everything.
